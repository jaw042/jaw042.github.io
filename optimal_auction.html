Optimal Auction Strategies

Project Overview

The optimal auction strategy project was done for an economics class at Northwestern. The idea was to determine how one should bid in an auction given the auction type, how many people are bidding, and a unique value for the item being sold. We ran a series of pseudo-auctions in our class to collect data and used it to draw conclusions about optimal strategies.

Auction Types

We tested several different types of auctions in our experiment:
  • First price auctions - highest bidder wins and pays their bid; the winner has a utility equal to their value minus their bid, everyone else has utility 0
  • Second price auctions - highest bidder wins and pays a price equal to the second highest bid; winner has a utility equal to their value minus the second highest bid, everyone else has a utility of 0
  • All pay auctions - highest bidder wins, but everyone pays their bid; the winner has a utility equal to their value minus their bid, everyone else has a utiilty equal to -bid
The optimal strategy for a second price auction is to bid your value, and this can be proven trivially. Additionally all pay auctions are somewhat rare, so for this overview, I'll focus on the first price auction piece of this experiment.

Setup and Theoretical Optimal Strategies

We operated our auctions under the following format:
  • Participants would be assigned a random, uniformly distributed value between 1 and 100 (inclusive)
  • Participants would be told how many people they were bidding against, but not specifically who
  • Participants would enter their bids, as well as their values and number of opponents, using an online form
Under these assumptions, we can derive a theoretical optimal bidding strategy as a function of the number of participants in the auction and the value assigned to any given participant. Results

The scatter plots on the left show the empirical expected utilities for different bids based on how participants actually bid. The graphs are for an auction size of 2, and for values of 10, 57 and 80 in that order. If you adjust the desmos window to similar settings, you'll find that the empirical graphs are pretty close to the theoretical predictions. In fact, we found that for an auction of size 2, the empirical optimal strategy was to bid 0.48*value, with a standard error of 0.026. For an auction of size 16, we found that the optimal strategy was to bid 0.93*value, with a standard error of 0.01. Both of these estimates are very close to the theoretical optimal bidding strategies, which is interesting because for the most part, people did not follow the optimal strategy. So, this suggests that regardless of other peoples' strategies, the theoretical optimal bidding strategyt is the way to go.
